The Madman HK

Lessons from a schizophrenic patient on understanding psychiatric conditions

Monomania—a fierce obsession with a single topic—was the diagnosis of the first psychiatric patient I encountered. Today, we recognize his condition as schizophrenia. Interestingly, I met this patient, HK, around the same time I read "The Professor and the Madman," the true story behind the Oxford Dictionary and a schizophrenic surgeon's substantial contributions to it.

Psychiatric patients, often misunderstood globally, face intense stigmatization, especially in Middle Eastern societies. HK was a heavy smoker, non-violent, and notably intelligent regarding pharmaceuticals. Uniquely, he was the only patient permitted to smoke in the hospital.

Once, HK explained to me that he began smoking in college, detailing nicotine's physiological benefits, especially for schizophrenics, whose bodies absorb nicotine more efficiently, creating a calming effect. Consequently, schizophrenic patients are typically heavy smokers, surpassing all other psychiatric demographics.

Unlike most patients unaware of smoking's mitigating effects on cognitive symptoms and auditory stimuli, HK knew precisely why nicotine helped him. When denied a lighter, he pleaded for nicotine patches, fully aware of nicotine's neurobiological impacts.

Yet, this therapeutic habit results in higher respiratory disease rates among psychiatric patients. HK taught me it's insufficient merely to destigmatize psychiatric illnesses or romanticize differences; it is our responsibility to accommodate and fully understand these conditions.
