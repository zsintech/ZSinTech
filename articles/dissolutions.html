Dissolutions

On language, brain function, and the fragility of human cognition

It was through the loss of distinct language abilities that we discovered the linguistic landmarks within the anatomy of the human brain. Anyone familiar with an Oliver Sacks short story understands how heart-wrenching language dissolution is. Life is a common complexity, and precisely because we are familiar with its quirks, we become immune to itâ€”until we are robbed of the complexity of our achievements.

Different areas of the brain control various language functions. Broadly speaking, Broca's area handles speech production, while Wernicke's area manages language comprehension. Aphasia refers to the dissolution of language and speech resulting from injury, typically within the central area and left hemisphere of the brain. A significant issue arises here: among all human tissues, nerves in the central nervous system do not regenerate.

There are varying types of aphasia, differing in severity and often overlapping. Two primary types offer foundational explanations: Broca's aphasia, where the production of key words (subjects, verbs, and objects) becomes hesitant and inaccurate, yet comprehension remains relatively intact; and Wernicke's aphasia, which severely disrupts the ability to process conversational feedback.

Of course, dissolution isn't only caused by brain damage or inherited neurophysiological disorders that might result in childhood aphasia. However, I find myself torn by the profound learning we gain through the misfortune of others.
