The Stoic General

Lessons in duty and moral courage from Byzantine history

We can extract many moral lessons from history—an activity I particularly relish. Recently, I read about the life of Belisarius, the sole general trusted with the immense responsibility of restoring the Roman Empire to its former glory. Tasked with recapturing Rome—a vital symbol of empire—Belisarius faced numerous adversaries but initially retained the emperor's favor.

This historical figure stirred deep feelings of morality, bravery, and compassion in me. Belisarius marched toward Rome for two arduous years, employing strategic brilliance and cunning tactics. Yet, when victory was near, Emperor Justinian rescinded his support, demanding the general's return.

Belisarius refused to abandon his mission, persisting despite losing imperial favor. Offered kingship by the Goths, he seemingly accepted—but his heart remained loyal. Despite Justinian's lost trust, Belisarius intended to return Rome's crown and treasury immediately to the emperor, risking his reputation for honesty.

This general's unwavering sense of duty is inspiring. There will inevitably be moments when trivialities distract us, even loved ones divert us from the path we know to be right. When overwhelmed, remember Belisarius, who overcame starvation, conflict, and the loss of imperial friendship, steadfastly fulfilling his duty.

Then, take a deep breath and march onward.
